# Adam Optimizer

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) is one of the most popular and widely used optimization algorithms in deep learning. It was introduced by Diederik P. Kingma and Jimmy Ba in their 2015 paper "Adam: A Method for Stochastic Optimization".

## Key Idea

Adam combines the best properties of two other adaptive optimization algorithms: RMSProp and momentum. It maintains both:
1. An exponentially decaying average of past gradients (like momentum)
2. An exponentially decaying average of past squared gradients (like RMSProp)

This allows Adam to adapt the learning rate for each parameter while also incorporating momentum.

## Mathematical Formulation

The standard SGD update rule is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \cdot g_t$$

Where:
- $\theta_t$ represents the parameters at time step $t$
- $\eta$ is the learning rate
- $g_t$ is the gradient of the loss function with respect to the parameters at time step $t$

Adam modifies this update rule in several ways:

### 1. Compute Momentum (First Moment)

$$m_t = \beta_1 \cdot m_{t-1} + (1 - \beta_1) \cdot g_t$$

Where:
- $m_t$ is the estimate of the first moment (mean) of the gradients
- $\beta_1$ is the exponential decay rate for the first moment estimates (typically 0.9)

### 2. Compute RMSProp (Second Moment)

$$v_t = \beta_2 \cdot v_{t-1} + (1 - \beta_2) \cdot g_t^2$$

Where:
- $v_t$ is the estimate of the second moment (uncentered variance) of the gradients
- $\beta_2$ is the exponential decay rate for the second moment estimates (typically 0.999)

### 3. Bias Correction

Since $m_t$ and $v_t$ are initialized as zeros, they are biased toward zero, especially during the initial time steps. To counteract this, Adam applies bias correction:

$$\hat{m}_t = \frac{m_t}{1 - \beta_1^t}$$

$$\hat{v}_t = \frac{v_t}{1 - \beta_2^t}$$

Where $\beta_1^t$ and $\beta_2^t$ are $\beta_1$ and $\beta_2$ raised to the power of $t$.

### Understanding Bias Correction in Detail

Bias correction is a critical component of Adam that addresses a significant issue with exponential moving averages when initialized at zero. Let's explore why it's needed and what happens without it:

#### Why Bias Correction is Necessary

When we initialize $m_0 = 0$ and $v_0 = 0$ and use exponential moving averages, our estimates are biased toward zero in the early iterations. This happens because:

1. For the first moment ($m_t$), with $\beta_1 = 0.9$:
   - At $t=1$: $m_1 = 0.9 \times 0 + 0.1 \times g_1 = 0.1 \times g_1$
   - At $t=2$: $m_2 = 0.9 \times 0.1g_1 + 0.1 \times g_2 = 0.09g_1 + 0.1g_2$
   - At $t=3$: $m_3 = 0.9 \times (0.09g_1 + 0.1g_2) + 0.1 \times g_3 = 0.081g_1 + 0.09g_2 + 0.1g_3$

2. For the second moment ($v_t$), with $\beta_2 = 0.999$:
   - At $t=1$: $v_1 = 0.999 \times 0 + 0.001 \times g_1^2 = 0.001 \times g_1^2$
   - At $t=2$: $v_2 = 0.999 \times 0.001g_1^2 + 0.001 \times g_2^2 = 0.000999g_1^2 + 0.001g_2^2$

The problem is that these estimates are significantly smaller than the true expected values. For example, if gradients were constant at $g$, the expected value of $m_t$ would eventually converge to $g$, but in early iterations, it's much smaller.

#### Mathematical Explanation of the Bias

For a constant gradient $g$, the expected value of the first moment without bias correction would be:

$$E[m_t] = (1 - \beta_1) \sum_{i=1}^{t} \beta_1^{t-i} g = (1 - \beta_1) g \sum_{i=1}^{t} \beta_1^{t-i} = (1 - \beta_1) g \frac{1 - \beta_1^t}{1 - \beta_1} = g(1 - \beta_1^t)$$

This shows that $m_t$ is biased by a factor of $(1 - \beta_1^t)$. Similarly, $v_t$ is biased by a factor of $(1 - \beta_2^t)$.

#### What Happens Without Bias Correction

Without bias correction, Adam would suffer from several issues:

1. **Slow Initial Progress**: In early iterations, the effective step sizes would be much smaller than intended because both $m_t$ and $v_t$ are biased toward zero.

2. **Inconsistent Behavior**: The algorithm would behave differently in early iterations compared to later ones, making it harder to tune hyperparameters effectively.

3. **Delayed Adaptation**: The adaptive learning rate mechanism would take longer to become effective, potentially slowing convergence.

4. **Practical Example**: In our numerical example with $f(x) = x^2 + 10$, without bias correction, the first update would be approximately 10 times smaller than with bias correction, resulting in much slower initial progress.

#### Visualizing the Effect of Bias Correction

Let's compare the effective step sizes with and without bias correction for the first few iterations (assuming constant gradient for simplicity):

| Iteration | With Bias Correction | Without Bias Correction | Ratio |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------------------|-------|
| 1         | $\eta \cdot g$       | $\eta \cdot 0.1g$        | 10x   |
| 2         | $\eta \cdot g$       | $\eta \cdot 0.19g$       | 5.26x |
| 3         | $\eta \cdot g$       | $\eta \cdot 0.271g$      | 3.69x |
| 10        | $\eta \cdot g$       | $\eta \cdot 0.651g$      | 1.54x |
| 100       | $\eta \cdot g$       | $\eta \cdot 0.9999g$     | 1.00x |

As $t$ increases, the bias becomes negligible, but in early iterations, it significantly affects the algorithm's behavior.

#### Implementation Note

The bias correction terms $(1 - \beta_1^t)$ and $(1 - \beta_2^t)$ approach 1 as $t$ increases, making the correction less significant for later iterations. This is why bias correction is particularly important during the early stages of training.

### 4. Parameter Update Rule

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \epsilon} \cdot \hat{m}_t$$

Where:
- $\epsilon$ is a small constant for numerical stability (typically 1e-8)

## How Adam Improves on Previous Optimizers

1. **Combines Momentum and RMSProp**: Adam incorporates the benefits of both momentum (which helps accelerate convergence) and RMSProp (which adapts learning rates based on gradient history).

2. **Bias Correction**: The bias correction terms help Adam perform better in the early stages of training.

3. **Adaptive Learning Rates**: Like RMSProp and Adagrad, Adam adapts the learning rate for each parameter, which is beneficial for dealing with sparse gradients or noisy data.

4. **Robust to Hyperparameter Choice**: Adam is often less sensitive to the choice of initial learning rate than other optimizers.

## Advantages

1. **Efficient Computation**: Despite maintaining two moving averages, Adam is computationally efficient.

2. **Low Memory Requirements**: Requires only a modest increase in memory compared to simpler optimizers.

3. **Suitable for Non-Stationary Objectives**: Works well for problems with very noisy or sparse gradients.

4. **Handles Large Datasets**: Performs well on large datasets and high-dimensional parameter spaces.

5. **Default Choice**: Often considered a good default optimizer for many deep learning applications.

## Disadvantages

1. **Generalization Issues**: Some research suggests Adam may generalize worse than SGD with momentum in some cases.

2. **Convergence Issues**: In certain scenarios, Adam might not converge to the optimal solution.

3. **Learning Rate Tuning**: While less sensitive than other methods, the learning rate may still require tuning for optimal performance.

## Variants of Adam

### AdamW

AdamW is a variant that implements weight decay correctly, addressing an issue in the original Adam implementation where L2 regularization was less effective.

### AMSGrad

AMSGrad is a variant that maintains the maximum of all past squared gradients $v_t$ and uses this maximum value for normalizing the gradients. This helps address some of the convergence issues in the original Adam algorithm.

## When to Use Adam

Adam is particularly well-suited for:

- Training deep neural networks
- Problems with noisy or sparse gradients
- Large datasets and high-dimensional parameter spaces
- When you need a good default optimizer with minimal tuning

## Comparison with Other Optimizers

- **vs. SGD**: Adam typically converges faster than SGD, especially in the early stages of training, but SGD with proper learning rate scheduling might generalize better in some cases.
  
- **vs. RMSProp**: Adam adds momentum and bias correction to RMSProp, which often leads to better performance.
  
- **vs. Adadelta**: Adam typically converges faster than Adadelta and is less sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters.

## Simple Example

Here's a step-by-step example of how Adam works:

1. Initialize parameters $\theta_0$, moments $m_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 0$, and time step $t = 0$
2. At each time step $t$:
   - Compute gradient $g_t$
   - Update biased first moment: $m_t = 0.9 \times m_{t-1} + 0.1 \times g_t$
   - Update biased second moment: $v_t = 0.999 \times v_{t-1} + 0.001 \times g_t^2$
   - Compute bias-corrected first moment: $\hat{m}_t = m_t / (1 - 0.9^t)$
   - Compute bias-corrected second moment: $\hat{v}_t = v_t / (1 - 0.999^t)$
   - Update parameters: $\theta_t = \theta_{t-1} - 0.001 \times \hat{m}_t / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + 10^{-8})$

This process continues iteratively, with Adam automatically adjusting the effective learning rate for each parameter based on the first and second moments of the gradients.

## Numerical Example: Minimizing a Simple Function

Let's walk through a concrete example of using Adam to minimize the simple function:

$$f(x) = x^2 + 10$$

This is a simple parabola with a global minimum at $x = 0$, where $f(0) = 10$.

The derivative of this function is:

$$f'(x) = 2x$$

Now let's apply Adam to find the minimum:

### Initial Setup
- Starting point: $x_0 = 5$
- Learning rate: $\eta = 0.1$
- First moment decay rate: $\beta_1 = 0.9$
- Second moment decay rate: $\beta_2 = 0.999$
- Small constant: $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$
- First moment: $m_0 = 0$
- Second moment: $v_0 = 0$
- Time step: $t = 0$

### Iteration 1 (t = 1)
- Current position: $x_0 = 5$
- Gradient: $g_1 = f'(x_0) = 2 \times 5 = 10$
- Update first moment: $m_1 = \beta_1 \times m_0 + (1 - \beta_1) \times g_1 = 0.9 \times 0 + 0.1 \times 10 = 1$
- Update second moment: $v_1 = \beta_2 \times v_0 + (1 - \beta_2) \times g_1^2 = 0.999 \times 0 + 0.001 \times 10^2 = 0.1$
- Bias-corrected first moment: $\hat{m}_1 = \frac{m_1}{1 - \beta_1^1} = \frac{1}{1 - 0.9} = 10$
- Bias-corrected second moment: $\hat{v}_1 = \frac{v_1}{1 - \beta_2^1} = \frac{0.1}{1 - 0.999} = 100$
- Update: $x_1 = x_0 - \frac{\eta \times \hat{m}_1}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_1} + \epsilon} = 5 - \frac{0.1 \times 10}{\sqrt{100} + 10^{-8}} = 5 - \frac{1}{10} = 4.9$
- New function value: $f(x_1) = 4.9^2 + 10 = 24.01 + 10 = 34.01$

### Iteration 2 (t = 2)
- Current position: $x_1 = 4.9$
- Gradient: $g_2 = f'(x_1) = 2 \times 4.9 = 9.8$
- Update first moment: $m_2 = \beta_1 \times m_1 + (1 - \beta_1) \times g_2 = 0.9 \times 1 + 0.1 \times 9.8 = 0.9 + 0.98 = 1.88$
- Update second moment: $v_2 = \beta_2 \times v_1 + (1 - \beta_2) \times g_2^2 = 0.999 \times 0.1 + 0.001 \times 9.8^2 = 0.0999 + 0.096 = 0.1959$
- Bias-corrected first moment: $\hat{m}_2 = \frac{m_2}{1 - \beta_1^2} = \frac{1.88}{1 - 0.9^2} = \frac{1.88}{1 - 0.81} = \frac{1.88}{0.19} = 9.89$
- Bias-corrected second moment: $\hat{v}_2 = \frac{v_2}{1 - \beta_2^2} = \frac{0.1959}{1 - 0.999^2} = \frac{0.1959}{1 - 0.998} = \frac{0.1959}{0.002} = 97.95$
- Update: $x_2 = x_1 - \frac{\eta \times \hat{m}_2}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_2} + \epsilon} = 4.9 - \frac{0.1 \times 9.89}{\sqrt{97.95} + 10^{-8}} = 4.9 - \frac{0.989}{9.897} = 4.9 - 0.1 = 4.8$
- New function value: $f(x_2) = 4.8^2 + 10 = 23.04 + 10 = 33.04$

### Iteration 3 (t = 3)
- Current position: $x_2 = 4.8$
- Gradient: $g_3 = f'(x_2) = 2 \times 4.8 = 9.6$
- Update first moment: $m_3 = \beta_1 \times m_2 + (1 - \beta_1) \times g_3 = 0.9 \times 1.88 + 0.1 \times 9.6 = 1.692 + 0.96 = 2.652$
- Update second moment: $v_3 = \beta_2 \times v_2 + (1 - \beta_2) \times g_3^2 = 0.999 \times 0.1959 + 0.001 \times 9.6^2 = 0.1957 + 0.092 = 0.2877$
- Bias-corrected first moment: $\hat{m}_3 = \frac{m_3}{1 - \beta_1^3} = \frac{2.652}{1 - 0.9^3} = \frac{2.652}{1 - 0.729} = \frac{2.652}{0.271} = 9.79$
- Bias-corrected second moment: $\hat{v}_3 = \frac{v_3}{1 - \beta_2^3} = \frac{0.2877}{1 - 0.999^3} = \frac{0.2877}{1 - 0.997} = \frac{0.2877}{0.003} = 95.9$
- Update: $x_3 = x_2 - \frac{\eta \times \hat{m}_3}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_3} + \epsilon} = 4.8 - \frac{0.1 \times 9.79}{\sqrt{95.9} + 10^{-8}} = 4.8 - \frac{0.979}{9.793} = 4.8 - 0.1 = 4.7$
- New function value: $f(x_3) = 4.7^2 + 10 = 22.09 + 10 = 32.09$

### Key Observations About Adam

Notice several important aspects of how Adam works:

1. **Momentum**: The first moment (m) acts like momentum, helping to maintain consistent direction of updates even when gradients fluctuate.

2. **Adaptive Learning Rates**: The second moment (v) adapts the learning rate for each parameter based on the history of squared gradients.

3. **Bias Correction**: The bias correction terms are particularly important in the early iterations, preventing the algorithm from making too small updates initially.

4. **Consistent Progress**: Adam makes consistent progress toward the minimum, with each step being approximately the same size in this simple example.

5. **Efficiency**: Even with the additional computations for the first and second moments, Adam efficiently moves toward the minimum.
