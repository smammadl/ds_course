# Moving Averages: Mathematical Formulas and Applications

Moving averages are statistical calculations used to analyze data points by creating a series of averages of different subsets of the full data set. They are widely used in time series analysis, signal processing, and financial applications to smooth out short-term fluctuations and highlight longer-term trends or cycles.

## Types of Moving Averages

### 1. Simple Moving Average (SMA)

#### Mathematical Formula
For a time series $X = \{x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\}$ and a window size $w$, the Simple Moving Average at time $t$ is:

$$\text{SMA}_t = \frac{1}{w} \sum_{i=t-w+1}^{t} x_i = \frac{x_{t-w+1} + x_{t-w+2} + ... + x_t}{w}$$

#### Example
For the data series $[1, 3, 5, 8, 12, 18, 20, 22, 25]$ with a window size of 3:

- $\text{SMA}_3 = \frac{1 + 3 + 5}{3} = 3.0$
- $\text{SMA}_4 = \frac{3 + 5 + 8}{3} = 5.33$
- $\text{SMA}_5 = \frac{5 + 8 + 12}{3} = 8.33$
- $\text{SMA}_6 = \frac{8 + 12 + 18}{3} = 12.67$
- $\text{SMA}_7 = \frac{12 + 18 + 20}{3} = 16.67$
- $\text{SMA}_8 = \frac{18 + 20 + 22}{3} = 20.0$
- $\text{SMA}_9 = \frac{20 + 22 + 25}{3} = 22.33$

### 2. Weighted Moving Average (WMA)

#### Mathematical Formula
For a time series $X = \{x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\}$, a window size $w$, and a set of weights $W = \{w_1, w_2, ..., w_w\}$ where $\sum_{i=1}^{w} w_i = 1$, the Weighted Moving Average at time $t$ is:

$$\text{WMA}_t = \sum_{i=1}^{w} w_i \cdot x_{t-w+i}$$

A common weighting scheme is linear weighting, where more recent values have higher weights:

$$\text{WMA}_t = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{w} i \cdot x_{t-w+i}}{\sum_{i=1}^{w} i}$$

#### Normalizing Weights

In practice, weights often need to be normalized to ensure they sum to 1, which maintains the scale of the original data and makes the moving average more interpretable. The normalization process converts any set of weights to a proper probability distribution.

Given a set of raw weights $R = \{r_1, r_2, ..., r_w\}$, the normalized weights $W = \{w_1, w_2, ..., w_w\}$ are calculated as:

$$w_i = \frac{r_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{w} r_j}$$

For example, with linear weights for a window size of 3, the raw weights would be $R = \{1, 2, 3\}$. The normalization process would be:

$$w_1 = \frac{1}{1+2+3} = \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.167$$
$$w_2 = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333$$
$$w_3 = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$$

The normalized weights $W = \{0.167, 0.333, 0.5\}$ sum to 1, ensuring that the weighted average maintains the scale of the original data.

Other common weighting schemes include:

1. **Exponential weights**: $R = \{e^0, e^1, e^2, ..., e^{w-1}\}$
2. **Gaussian weights**: $R = \{e^{-\frac{(i-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\}$ for $i = 1,2,...,w$
3. **Custom domain-specific weights**: Based on specific knowledge about the importance of each observation

In all cases, these raw weights are normalized using the formula above to ensure they sum to 1.

#### Example
For the data series $[1, 3, 5, 8, 12, 18, 20, 22, 25]$ with a window size of 3 and weights $[1, 2, 3]$ (normalized to sum to 1, these would be $[\frac{1}{6}, \frac{2}{6}, \frac{3}{6}]$):

- $\text{WMA}_3 = \frac{1 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 3 + 3 \cdot 5}{1 + 2 + 3} = \frac{24}{6} = 4.0$
- $\text{WMA}_4 = \frac{1 \cdot 3 + 2 \cdot 5 + 3 \cdot 8}{6} = \frac{37}{6} \approx 6.17$
- $\text{WMA}_5 = \frac{1 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 8 + 3 \cdot 12}{6} = \frac{57}{6} = 9.5$
- $\text{WMA}_6 = \frac{1 \cdot 8 + 2 \cdot 12 + 3 \cdot 18}{6} = \frac{86}{6} \approx 14.33$
- $\text{WMA}_7 = \frac{1 \cdot 12 + 2 \cdot 18 + 3 \cdot 20}{6} = \frac{108}{6} = 18.0$
- $\text{WMA}_8 = \frac{1 \cdot 18 + 2 \cdot 20 + 3 \cdot 22}{6} = \frac{124}{6} \approx 20.67$
- $\text{WMA}_9 = \frac{1 \cdot 20 + 2 \cdot 22 + 3 \cdot 25}{6} = \frac{139}{6} \approx 23.17$

### 3. Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

#### Mathematical Formula
For a time series $X = \{x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\}$ and a smoothing factor $\alpha$ (where $0 < \alpha < 1$), the Exponential Moving Average at time $t$ is:

$$\text{EMA}_t = \alpha \cdot x_t + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \text{EMA}_{t-1}$$

With the initial value typically set as $\text{EMA}_1 = x_1$.

The smoothing factor $\alpha$ is often calculated as $\alpha = \frac{2}{N+1}$, where $N$ is the span (equivalent window size).

#### Example
For the data series $[1, 3, 5, 8, 12, 18, 20, 22, 25]$ with a span of 3 ($\alpha = \frac{2}{3+1} = 0.5$):

- $\text{EMA}_1 = 1$ (initial value)
- $\text{EMA}_2 = 0.5 \cdot 3 + 0.5 \cdot 1 = 2.0$
- $\text{EMA}_3 = 0.5 \cdot 5 + 0.5 \cdot 2 = 3.5$
- $\text{EMA}_4 = 0.5 \cdot 8 + 0.5 \cdot 3.5 = 5.75$
- $\text{EMA}_5 = 0.5 \cdot 12 + 0.5 \cdot 5.75 = 8.875$
- $\text{EMA}_6 = 0.5 \cdot 18 + 0.5 \cdot 8.875 = 13.4375$
- $\text{EMA}_7 = 0.5 \cdot 20 + 0.5 \cdot 13.4375 = 16.71875$
- $\text{EMA}_8 = 0.5 \cdot 22 + 0.5 \cdot 16.71875 = 19.359375$
- $\text{EMA}_9 = 0.5 \cdot 25 + 0.5 \cdot 19.359375 = 22.1796875$

## Connection to First and Second Moments

### First Moment (Mean)

The first moment of a distribution is its mean or expected value. Moving averages, particularly the SMA, are direct estimators of the local first moment of a time series. They provide an estimate of the central tendency of the data within the window.

- **Simple Moving Average (SMA)**: Directly estimates the first moment (mean) of the data within the window.
- **Weighted Moving Average (WMA)**: Estimates a weighted first moment, giving more importance to certain observations (typically more recent ones).
- **Exponential Moving Average (EMA)**: Estimates an exponentially weighted first moment, with exponentially decreasing weights for older observations.

### Second Moment and Variance

The second moment is related to the variance and spread of the data. While moving averages themselves estimate the first moment, they can be used to calculate moving variance and standard deviation, which are measures of the second moment.

#### Moving Variance
For a window of size $w$, the moving variance at time $t$ can be calculated as:

$$\text{Moving Variance}_t = \frac{1}{w} \sum_{i=t-w+1}^{t} (x_i - \text{SMA}_t)^2$$

This is an estimate of the local second central moment of the time series.

### Applications in Optimization and Machine Learning

In optimization algorithms like Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), moving averages of the first and second moments of gradients are used to adapt learning rates and improve convergence:

1. **First Moment Estimation**: Algorithms like Momentum and Adam use exponential moving averages of gradients (first moment) to smooth out updates and accelerate convergence in the relevant direction.

2. **Second Moment Estimation**: Algorithms like RMSprop and Adam use exponential moving averages of squared gradients (second moment) to adapt learning rates for each parameter based on the historical volatility of its gradients.

For example, in the Adam optimizer:
- $m_t = \beta_1 \cdot m_{t-1} + (1 - \beta_1) \cdot g_t$ (first moment estimate)
- $v_t = \beta_2 \cdot v_{t-1} + (1 - \beta_2) \cdot g_t^2$ (second moment estimate)

Where $g_t$ is the gradient at time $t$, and $\beta_1$ and $\beta_2$ are decay rates for the moment estimates.

## Practical Applications

1. **Financial Markets**: Moving averages are extensively used in technical analysis to identify trends and generate trading signals.

2. **Signal Processing**: Used to filter out noise and extract meaningful patterns from signals.

3. **Time Series Forecasting**: Moving averages serve as simple forecasting models and components in more complex models like ARIMA.

4. **Machine Learning**: Used in optimization algorithms and as features for predictive models.

5. **Control Systems**: Used to smooth sensor readings and control signals in feedback systems.

Moving averages provide a versatile and computationally efficient way to analyze time-varying data across numerous domains, balancing the need to capture meaningful trends while reducing the impact of noise and outliers.
